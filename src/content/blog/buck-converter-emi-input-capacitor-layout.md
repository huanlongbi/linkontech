This example shows how different layout methods in buck-converter EMI design can affect interference performance.

The test circuit used the RT7297CHZSP buck regulator operating at 800 kHz with a 12 V input and 3.3 V / 3 A output.

The test circuit is shown in the figure below.

![Buck converter test circuit](/images/blog/buck-converter-emi-1.png)

The PCB used for testing allowed different layout options, including:

- PCB with or without ground plane
- different C_IN locations
- optional RC snubber
- optional Rboot resistor
- optional output filter

The EMI test board is shown in the figure below.

![EMI test board with multiple layout options](/images/blog/buck-converter-emi-2.png)

The EMI measurement setup is shown below.

A spectrum analyzer was used together with a homemade common-mode current probe installed on the input cable.

![EMI measurement setup](/images/blog/buck-converter-emi-3.png)

During the test, high-frequency switching current generated by the PCB creates radiated EMI.

These radiated fields couple into nearby cables and wiring, producing common-mode current on the input cable.

This common-mode current can then be measured by the current probe.

The common-mode current path is shown in the figure below.

![Common-mode current caused by radiated field](/images/blog/buck-converter-emi-4.png)

One side of the input battery ground was connected to the laboratory bench ground through a 100 Ω resistor.

This allowed the whole circuit to maintain a reference ground whose impedance is similar to the LISN network used in EMC testing.

## Experiment 1: C_IN placed far away from the IC

In the first experiment, C_IN was intentionally placed far away from the IC.

This created a very large high-current input loop between:

- C_IN
- VIN pin
- switching MOSFET
- return path

A larger loop generates a stronger magnetic field and usually leads to higher EMI.

The large input loop is shown in the figure below.

![Large input loop caused by distant capacitor placement](/images/blog/buck-converter-emi-5.png)

The common-mode current measured on the input cable was very strong across a wide frequency range.



![Measured common-mode current on the input cable](/images/blog/buck-converter-emi-6.png)

Using a small loop antenna above the PCB, the strongest radiated field was found near the large input loop area.

The switching waveform showed very high overshoot and ringing, and these peaks were already beyond the IC’s voltage rating.

The measured radiation and ringing are shown in the figure below.

![Radiation and ringing caused by the large input loop](/images/blog/buck-converter-emi-7.png)

The same large-loop layout was then tested again on a double-sided PCB with a ground plane.

Compared with the single-layer PCB, both the radiated EMI and the ringing amplitude became smaller.

The improvement provided by the ground plane is shown in the figure below.

![Effect of ground plane on EMI reduction](/images/blog/buck-converter-emi-8.png)

The reason is that the high-frequency current flowing in the large loop creates magnetic fields, while the return current in the ground plane creates an opposite magnetic field.

These fields partially cancel each other.

The closer the return path is to the current loop, the better the EMI reduction effect.

## Experiment 2: Move C_IN closer to the IC

The next step was to move C_IN much closer to the IC.

This reduced the size of the input loop significantly.

The improved C_IN placement is shown in the figure below.

![Smaller input loop with improved capacitor placement](/images/blog/buck-converter-emi-9.png)

The results improved immediately:

- ringing amplitude reduced by about 50%
- radiated EMI dropped by around 10 dB
- ringing bandwidth reduced to below 300 MHz

The measured waveform and EMI result are shown in the figure below.

![Waveform and EMI improvement after moving the capacitor](/images/blog/buck-converter-emi-10.png)

This experiment shows that improving C_IN placement is one of the most effective ways to reduce ringing and EMI.

## Experiment 3: Add a small 10 nF capacitor directly between VIN and GND

The most important conclusion from the previous experiment is that placing C_IN more effectively can reduce the amplitude of overshoot and ringing on the switching waveform, while also lowering high-frequency radiation.

In the RT7297CHZSP, the exposed thermal pad on the bottom of the package is not connected to the die.

Therefore, in PCB layout, connecting the copper area directly to the thermal pad does not shorten the C_IN loop.

The high-side MOSFET and low-side MOSFET are connected to the VIN and GND pins through multiple bond wires, so the shortest loop can only be formed through these two pins.

The internal connection structure is shown in the figure below.

![Thermal pad not connected internally to the die](/images/blog/buck-converter-emi-11.png)

Based on this conclusion, an additional 10 nF 0603 capacitor was placed directly between the VIN and GND pins of the IC.

This created the shortest possible high-frequency current loop using:

- VIN pin
- GND pin
- bond wires inside the IC
- short PCB traces
- a small ceramic capacitor

The added 10 nF capacitor is shown in the figure below.

![Adding a 10 nF capacitor directly between VIN and GND](/images/blog/buck-converter-emi-12.png)

After adding the capacitor, the high-frequency overshoot on the switching waveform disappeared.

However, low-frequency ringing was still present, and the antenna measurement showed that high-frequency radiation had been suppressed while a large low-frequency peak appeared around 25 MHz.

This low-frequency peak is caused by resonance between the original input capacitor and the newly added 10 nF capacitor.

The resonance loop includes:

- the 10 nF capacitor
- the main input capacitor
- PCB trace inductance
- bond-wire inductance inside the IC

In this test, the resonance frequency was around 25 MHz, corresponding to approximately 10 nH of equivalent inductance.

One way to suppress this resonance is to place a 22 µF / 1206 capacitor with relatively higher ESR in parallel with the 10 nF capacitor.

The optimized PCB layout is shown below.

![Optimized PCB layout for minimum input loop](/images/blog/buck-converter-emi-13.png)

With the optimized C_IN placement, the overshoot on the single-layer PCB disappeared completely.

The radiated noise measured by the loop antenna became very low, and the FFT result was close to the noise floor.

When the common-mode current on the input cable was measured again, the difference at some frequencies exceeded 30 dB, showing that the original noise problem had been greatly reduced.

The final result is shown in the figure below.

![Final optimized result](/images/blog/buck-converter-emi-14.png)

## Conclusion

For buck converters, C_IN placement has a major effect on EMI performance.

A large input loop creates stronger magnetic fields, larger common-mode current, and more ringing.

Moving C_IN closer to the IC and reducing the loop area can dramatically improve EMI performance without adding extra filters or shielding.

In many cases, optimizing the PCB layout is faster, cheaper, and more effective than adding more EMI components.
